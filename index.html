Understanding Professional Boundaries

Click inside the presentation, then use the arrow keys to move through the slides. Use the down arrow where the lesson includes vertical detail slides.

Professional boundaries are the clear limits that keep the helping relationship safe, ethical, and focused on the client’s recovery. They help staff provide warm, consistent care without turning the therapeutic relationship into a friendship, rescue role, or personal relationship.

Why this matters

Recovery work often involves trust, vulnerability, crisis, gratitude, and emotional intensity. Those are exactly the conditions where boundaries can blur. A healthy boundary protects both the client and the counselor while keeping the work focused on the client’s goals, safety, and recovery.

Common boundary risks

  • Sharing personal phone numbers or social media accounts
  • Giving or accepting gifts, money, rides, housing, or personal favors
  • Treating one client as “special” or making exceptions that would not apply to others
  • Keeping secrets from the treatment team or supervisor
  • Using self-disclosure in a way that shifts attention from the client to the staff member
Core practice: When a boundary question comes up, validate the client’s need, state the boundary clearly, redirect them to an approved support path, and consult or document when needed.

Therapeutic middle ground

The goal is neither cold distance nor over-involvement. The goal is a therapeutic middle ground: warm but professional, caring but boundaried, empathetic but not enmeshed.

Key principles

  • Maintain consistent expectations across clients
  • Use approved communication channels
  • Document boundary concerns promptly and factually
  • Seek supervision when uncertain
  • Ask: “Would I be comfortable if my supervisor read this in the chart?”
