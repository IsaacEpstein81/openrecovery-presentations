Why this matters

Early support can reduce stigma and help people connect to care sooner.

Mental Health First Aid gives everyday helpers a starting point. It helps you notice change, listen with care, and support an appropriate next step.

Human impact: a calm approach can reduce shame and fear.

Community impact: First Aiders strengthen schools, workplaces, teams, and neighborhoods.

Recovery impact: early support can help someone reach care or safer ground sooner.

Mental Health First Aid starts with how we notice, listen, and respond when someone may be struggling.

Start with the definition

Mental Health First Aid is initial help and support, not diagnosis or treatment.

health_and_safety

What it is

Short-term support for someone experiencing a mental health or substance use challenge, or a crisis, until professional help arrives or the situation becomes safer.

rule

What it is not

It is not therapy, a clinical assessment, or a replacement for emergency response, legal duties, or licensed treatment.

The core mindset is simple: stay grounded, reduce harm, and help the person move toward appropriate support.

The role in practice

First Aiders usually do three things well: notice, listen, and connect.

visibility

Notice meaningful change

Pay attention to shifts in behavior, thoughts, feelings, appearance, functioning, or safety that seem stronger or more sustained than usual.

hearing

Listen nonjudgmentally

Offer calm presence, open questions, and reassurance without minimizing, arguing, or taking over the person's experience.

groups

Link to support

Respect privacy, stay within your role, and help the person move toward appropriate resources, emergency help, or trusted supports.

How the course frames support

Respectful language and equity-aware practice help people feel seen, not reduced to a label.

Stigma harms: negative assumptions can lead to shame, silence, discrimination, and delayed help-seeking.

Person-first language matters: say "person living with depression" instead of letting a diagnosis stand in for the whole person.

Context matters too: this curriculum asks you to avoid stereotypes, notice structural barriers, and remember that scenarios represent people, not categories.

A respectful response avoids assumptions and keeps the person's dignity at the center of the conversation.

Shared language

Mental health and a mental health challenge are related, but they are not the same thing.

Mental health

The day-to-day ability to cope, function, build relationships, and contribute in ways that feel manageable and meaningful.

Mental health challenge

A stronger or longer-lasting change that interferes with daily life, relationships, work, learning, or safety.

First Aiders are not asked to label every feeling. They are asked to notice when a shift seems significant, sustained, or disruptive enough to deserve closer attention and support.

Why early support matters

Mental health challenges are common, and many people still face barriers to getting help.

1 in 5 U.S. adults

NIMH estimates that more than one in five U.S. adults lived with a mental illness in 2022.

Almost half in a lifetime

Mental Health First Aid notes that nearly half of American adults will experience a mental illness during their lifetime.

Support can reduce barriers

Stigma, cost, access, and uncertainty delay care. Early, nonjudgmental support can make help feel more reachable.

Recovery mindset

Recovery is possible, and support should be grounded in hope.

Recovery is a process that may involve care, community, practical support, and time. First Aiders help by reinforcing hope and connection.

Health: care and coping support day-to-day wellbeing.

Home: stable, safe living conditions support healing.

Purpose: work, learning, and meaningful goals can be part of recovery.

Community: relationships and belonging help recovery feel possible.

A recovery-oriented response keeps hope, dignity, and connection visible even when someone is struggling.

Quick orientation

Mental Health First Aid covers several common challenge areas where early support can make a difference.

Depression

Watch for persistent low mood, loss of interest, hopelessness, or changes in energy, sleep, and daily functioning.

Anxiety

Excessive worry, panic, physical tension, and avoidance can all shape how someone shows up and functions.

Trauma and stress

Hypervigilance, emotional flooding, shutdown, sleep disruption, or sudden reactions can signal a person is overwhelmed.

Substance use or psychosis

Behavior that feels disorganized, unsafe, detached from reality, or strongly tied to substance use may require faster support and safety planning.

Practical application

What might a First Aider notice first?

A coworker who is usually engaged begins arriving late, looks exhausted, stops joining others at lunch, and says, "I do not know how much longer I can keep doing this."

Notice the change: behavior, appearance, energy, isolation, and distress cues all matter.

Start with presence: approach calmly, listen without judgment, and avoid rushing to fix everything.

Know what comes next: the next module introduces ALGEE so you can respond with more structure.

First Aiders often begin with a small but important moment: they notice a real change and choose to respond with care.

Safety comes first

Supportive conversation matters, but there are moments when urgent safety action matters more.

Stay within your role: Mental Health First Aid does not replace emergency response, clinical care, legal duties, or local policy.

Respect privacy: share information carefully and only with the people or services who need it for safety, support, or required action.

Escalate when needed: if safety changes quickly, move toward emergency or crisis support instead of waiting to see whether things settle on their own.

Immediate action examples

Medical emergencies, overdose, direct suicidal intent, or severe disorientation with immediate danger are not wait-and-see moments.

Call 911 for immediate medical danger. Call or text 988 for suicidal or emotional crisis support when urgent crisis help is needed.
